The Lover's Death

by Ricardo Demetillo

He who had lived the earth with a firm love
Is now, being infirm, laid in the earth
That covers him with green grass quietly.
Once when he walked the fields, he suddenly knelt
And with an avid gesture clasped the earth.
His sun-lit fingers sifted dust.

Lovers would write their incoherent view
On passionate pages; but he, on pads of meadow,
Wrote with his plow a tongue-tied love.
Fields understood, for when the harvest ripened,
Fruits lay like brown breast for his hands to pluck,
And he with lightness, touched each pregnant stalk.

His house was quiet, like the man who closed
The gate behind him when the lamp light glowed.
He knew no woman's touch except the earth's.
We thought it fitting that the sun should touch
With quiet fingers the rice-fronds in the field
When he, after a fever, gave himself to dusk.

We could not salvage breath, but we could swathe
His body and lay it in the earth he loved.
He may return and beckon from a sheaf.
His body and lay it in the earth he loved
He may return and beckon from a sheaf

Analysis

"The Lover's Death" by Ricardo Demetillo is a reflective and poignant poem about a man who deeply loved the earth, lived a life of simplicity, and found his peace within the natural world. The poem explores the relationship between life, love, and death through the imagery of nature, farming, and the eternal cycle of life. The man’s love for the earth becomes the central metaphor for his life and his passing.

Symbolism

  • Love for the Earth:

    • The speaker begins by describing the man as someone who "had lived the earth with a firm love," emphasizing that his life was grounded in a deep connection to nature. His love was not romantic in the typical sense, but rather a profound bond with the earth itself, symbolized by his relationship with the fields and the crops.

    • The man's act of kneeling and clasping the earth is a symbol of reverence and respect, not only for the land but for the natural processes that sustain life. His "sun-lit fingers sifted dust," which suggests an intimate relationship with the soil, further reinforcing the idea of the earth as a lover or partner.

  • Work and Simplicity:

    • The speaker describes how the man worked the land, "writing with his plow a tongue-tied love." This metaphor illustrates how the man expressed his love for life and the earth through his labor, which is simple yet profound. Unlike others who express their love through passionate words or romantic gestures, this man communicates through the act of working the land, planting, and harvesting.

    • The man’s house is described as "quiet", symbolizing a life free from distractions, with no "woman's touch except the earth's." This suggests that his entire existence was centered around the earth and his work, making the earth his true companion.

  • Death and Legacy:

    • The man eventually succumbs to a fever and dies. The community swathes his body and places it in the earth he loved. The poem portrays his death as a return to the earth, which he had always considered his true home.

    • The final image of the man "returning and beckoning from a sheaf" suggests that his spirit or essence lives on through the harvest. This echoes the idea of rebirth in nature, as the earth that nourished him will now nourish others, creating a cyclical relationship between life, death, and the land.

Themes

  • The Connection Between Life and Nature:

    The poem underscores the deep and abiding relationship between the man and the earth. The man’s love is not for a person, but for the earth itself, and in his death, he returns to that earth, symbolizing the cycle of life.

  • Simplicity and Contentment:

    The poem celebrates a life of simplicity, where true fulfillment is found in working with nature. The man’s quiet, humble life stands in contrast to the complexities and distractions of modern living.

  • Death as a Return to the Earth:

    The poem portrays death not as an end, but as a return to nature, emphasizing the idea that death is a natural part of the cycle of life. The man’s body being laid in the earth he loved suggests that death is not something to fear, but rather an integration back into the life force of the world.

  • Legacy:

    The man’s labor, his care for the land, and the harvest he helped to bring forth are part of his legacy. Even in death, he continues to give life, as the fruits of his labor nourish others. The earth is his lasting legacy.

Lessons

  • The Value of Simplicity:

    The poem teaches that there is dignity and beauty in a simple life, centered around meaningful work and a deep connection to the natural world. The man’s life is a reminder that fulfillment does not come from wealth or status, but from the work we do and the relationships we nurture.

  • Embracing the Natural Cycle:

    The poem emphasizes that death is part of the natural cycle of life, and in death, we may find peace in knowing that we return to the earth that nourished us. It suggests that there is a profound and peaceful acceptance in being a part of something larger, like the cycles of nature.

  • The Enduring Nature of Legacy:

    The man’s love for the earth and his work lives on after his death, reminding readers that our actions and the way we live our lives leave lasting impacts, whether through the people we influence or the environment we shape. The harvest that he cultivated continues to nourish and sustain life, just as our deeds continue to affect future generations.

Reader's POV

From a reader’s perspective, "The Lover’s Death" evokes deep emotions about the relationship between humanity and nature. The poem invites readers to reflect on how people often find peace and fulfillment in the simplest and most profound connections with the natural world. The man’s life, his love for the earth, and his eventual return to it can resonate with readers who appreciate nature or have experienced the loss of someone who lived a simple, humble life.
The man’s death is not tragic in the conventional sense but is portrayed as a natural conclusion to his life, one that is in harmony with the earth he cherished. This suggests a sense of peaceful acceptance in the face of death and highlights the timelessness of nature’s cycles. The reader might feel a sense of comfort in the idea that, like the crops that grow from the earth, life continues and returns in a new form.

Conclusion

In conclusion, "The Lover’s Death" is a reflective and meditative poem about the harmonious relationship between life, labor, and death. It teaches readers to embrace simplicity, honor the cycles of nature, and understand that our legacies are often rooted in the quiet, enduring love we have for the world around us.
